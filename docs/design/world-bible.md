# World & Story Bible — *The Drowned Chorus*

Status: **canonical** as of 2026-07-23 (rewritten for the v10.0 solo pivot and
the v12.0 Ascent; fleshed out 2026-07-23). This is the narrative and world
foundation the game's tone, content, and UX are built on. It supersedes the
placeholder "Project Meterfall" codename (PRD §18's open naming question is
resolved here: the game is **The Drowned Chorus**).

The world is built from the recorded setlist of the band **Inhalants** — the
game's world *is* those songs, sunk and given a place. (History: an earlier
draft of this bible cast four song-title heroes descending together; v10.0
retired that fiction. The protagonist is now solo, the journey is an **ascent**,
and those four old names survive only as the soundtrack's songs, §11. This
document reflects the shipped story.)

**How the story is told.** Almost none of what follows is ever spoken. The game
narrates through *place* (arena staging, §7), through *ground* (the footprint
trail, the tracking puzzle, §6.4), through ~40 found **echoes** (§8), and through
three lines at the very end (§9). This bible is the canon *beneath* all of that —
the iceberg under the waterline. When in doubt, the rule is the game's oldest
one: **found, not told.**

---

## 1. Logline

A drowned guitarist climbs up through a black ocean of bone and rusted
clockwork toward the surface, chasing the one thing louder than the misery song
that fills it — his lost son — and the only way up is to sing back on the beat.

## 2. Premise — the Ascent

At the bottom of a lightless sea lies a gothic world that refused to end. Time
broke here long ago — *"black clocks line the walls," "melting the clocks like
waterfalls"* — so nothing rots away and nothing rests. The whole drowned realm
hums one endless composition, **the drowned chorus**, and the chorus is what
keeps everything sinking, feeding, and awake. At the far end of it, up where the
water thins, stands its composer: **the Conductor**, forever *trying to describe
a sound* that will not resolve — and the drowning is only the side effect of a
piece that will not end.

**Mir** wakes lying on the ocean floor, in the **Fold**: a town whose people
pray to a massive obelisk they did not raise and cannot leave. He can hear the
chorus more clearly than the things that live down here — and hearing it, he can
*answer* it. To sing back *on the beat* is to push against the song; to miss is
to be pulled under.

Mir leaves the Fold and climbs — up the drowned Shelf, across the Breach where
the world crosses the waterline, out onto the hostile Scar of the surface, and
on toward the Stage. His toddler son **Nari** follows him out of the town. On the
surface, Nari is taken. From that moment the whole game is one thing: the fight
to climb the rest of the way and get him back.

## 3. The Chorus — what the world *is*

*(Canon subtext. Never stated outright; it's the physics the images obey.)*

Long ago — or outside of time, which here is the same thing — something sang a
note that did not resolve. The first phrase of a composition with no ending.
Because the chord never lands, **time cannot move forward**: that is why the
clocks melt and nothing rots, nothing heals, nothing rests. The unresolved note
is a kind of gravity. It pulls things *down* — world after world, age after age,
sinking into the black — and everything that sinks is added to the sound. The
drowned become the strata (bone and rusted clockwork), and each drowned voice is
pressed into service sustaining the note. **The chorus is the accumulated grief
of everything that ever drowned, held open, unable to finish.**

To hold the **baton** is to be the one *trying to describe the sound* — to find
the chord that finally lands and lets the piece, the world, and time itself
end. Conducting is not a throne; it is a sentence. You conduct until someone
takes the baton from you. The current holder is **the Conductor**.

Three things follow from this, and the whole game rests on them:

- **The chorus erases the moment of drowning.** It is the one mercy and the one
  theft: the drowned wake in the Fold not remembering going under. That is why
  the town can pray in peace to the thing that is drowning them.
- **To resonate against the note is to resist it.** A voice carrying its *own*
  unresolved chord — a grief that hasn't been let go — beats against the world's
  and refuses to be fully absorbed. This is what "singing back on the beat" is,
  mechanically and literally. It is also why Mir, of all the drowned, can climb.
- **The last chord is the ending.** Land it and the composition resolves — which
  means the world can finally *end*: not blown apart, but allowed to rest. Time
  moves. The water quiets. The drowned are released.

## 4. The rhythm is diegetic

The combat mechanic is the story. A battle is a passage of the drowned chorus;
every ability is a phrase sung into it. Hitting the beat (§8.3 in the PRD) is
**singing back** — countering the misery song; missing lets it take the phrase.
This is why the timing model is the game's spine, not a minigame:

- **Focus** — a breath held for a difficult line, spent on specials.
- **Groove** (the swelling meter) — the chorus turning in *Mir's* favour; spent
  in full on an **ultimate**, a verse loud enough to break the song.
- **Sightread** — literally *seeing the music*: a forecast of the next bars of
  the chorus and the strikes riding them (PRD §8.4's forecast assist).

Grief and rhythm are the same substance here. Mir fights by refusing to miss a
beat because that is exactly how he refuses to stop looking.

## 5. The four figures

Four people are caught on the note, and each answers grief a different way. That
is the spine of the whole story: **the same wound, four responses, one of them
right.**

### Mir — grief as *pursuit*

A guitarist from the world above. He drowned with his son, and the chorus took
the memory of it — he wakes on the ocean floor with no going-under, only an
after. What it could not take is the *reason*: he knows Nari was with him, and
that is the whole of what he knows.

He can sing back where the drowned cannot because he has not finished drowning.
His grief is a chord of his own, unresolved, and it will not lie flat against the
world's. As long as he is climbing toward his son he is not fully sunk — which is
why he is the first soul in living memory to walk *out* of the Fold. His kit
(light / heavy / special / ultimate / dash / parry, PRD §8.2) is verses sung
against the chorus; his ultimate is the loudest a single voice can get before the
song drowns it. He is not a chosen hero. He is a father who would not stop, and
in this world that is the rarest thing there is.

### Nari — the unwritten voice

Mir's toddler son. He drowned too — but a child who goes under still trailing a
parent is a particular thing here: **a voice the chorus has not yet assigned a
place.** An unwritten note. Everything else in the deep has long since been
pressed into the sound; Nari has not, because the Fold is deep and sheltered and
he was always at his father's heel. He follows Mir out of the town the way a
toddler follows — for no reason but that his father is walking.

He is taken on the surface, where the chorus can finally reach a voice the deep
kept from it. He is never an escort objective, an inventory item, or a fail
state (PRD §8.4). He is the *reason*, rendered entirely in the ground: a
footprint trail that ends at a scuffle, then sparse clues, then a cage. An
unwritten voice is the one thing that could complete the composition — or the one
thing pure enough to be worth stealing *out* of it. Both people who understand
that want him. Only one of them is his father.

### Lunal — grief as *keeping*

The masked huntress who holds the Conductor's strings. She conducted, once,
before he did — and she is the one who learned the truth first: **the piece will
never resolve.** Every ending she wrote, the world erased under her hands (the
melting clocks, the blank pages, the last row re-conducted forever). So she put
down the ending and picked up something worse. She stopped trying to *finish* the
song and started trying to *stop it from touching* the few things she could not
bear to lose.

She hunts, and she keeps. *"She doesn't hunt to kill. She hunts to keep."* She
collects the voices too precious to let sink into the chorus and hides them in
cages the song cannot rewrite — held exactly as they are, forever, safe from
being dissolved into the endless note. In her logic the cage is *mercy*: a boy
who stopped growing when the water came, kept whole, never to change, never to be
sung. Her horror is the horror of preservation — **love as taxidermy.** She keeps
the Conductor the same way: the previous baton-holder she could neither finish
nor free, now her prisoner and her instrument, kept conducting so the world stays
stable enough that nothing in her collection ever has to change. She took Nari to
save him. She is the villain, and she is not wrong that the surface would have
been worse. That is what makes her the villain.

She is revealed only through staging, at the finale — the mask clatters where she
stood (PRD §8.7). A playable Lunal confrontation after the Conductor is a v2
candidate (§18).

### The Conductor — grief as *endless revision*

The world's composer and final boss (PRD §8.7). Gaunt, black-coated, a clock
where a heart should tick, a baton raised over a drowned orchestra of the things
Mir fought to get here. He is not evil so much as *unfinished*. He genuinely
wants to land the last chord — to describe the sound at last and let it *all*
end, mercifully, so everything can rest. He cannot, and Lunal will not let him
stop trying, because his trying is the load-bearing wall of her whole still
world. He re-conducts the moment of loss forever, erasing every attempt but the
one that came closest, keeping the players who got it right because he could
never let anything that near the ending leave.

His three phases are three real attempts at that ending: a stable **4/4** that
fractures into **3/4**, then a desperate cycle of **5/4 → 7/8 → 4/4 → 3/4** as
the composition comes apart in his hands. The music falters every time the small
one in the cage cries — then starts again, angrier: he is trying to finish over
the sound of the one voice that could actually finish it. Beat him and the baton
is free.

## 6. The Ascent — regions as movements, and as a grief-arc

The campaign graph (`opening_biome.json`) is one continuous climb from the
seafloor to the Stage: **20 fight-nodes** across five regions, and the road tours
the whole world before it arrives. Each region is a movement of the chorus, a
stage of the climb, and — read one layer down — a stage of grief. Combat begins
only once Mir leaves the Fold; the generator *asserts* no fight lands inside the
sanctuary.

| Region | Movement | Grief-stage | Staged as | Foe |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **The Fold** | *waking on the floor* | denial — the sheltered deep | the drowned harvest village | *none — Nari still follows* |
| **The Kelp Shelf** | *the climb* | bargaining — "if you don't weigh anything anymore" | the drowned salt mine | salt-wracked drifters |
| **The Breach** | *crossing the waterline* | the wound — where Nari is taken | the drowned carnival | drifter packs |
| **The Scar** | *the hostile surface* | despair — the long search into dread | the collector's den | elite wraiths |
| **The Conductor's Stage** | *the ending he rehearses* | acceptance — keep him, or let the chord land | the drowned concert hall | **the Conductor** (Lunal behind him) |

### 6.1 The Fold — denial

Mir wakes on the silt among people who pray to a massive obelisk they did not
raise, cannot explain, and will never leave. The obelisk is the chorus made
visible — *the thing that was already listening when they woke* — and the town's
peace is the peace of not knowing you have drowned. Nobody leaves the Fold; it
isn't a law, it's just that no one ever has, because leaving means remembering
you have somewhere to leave *from*. Mir leaves because the chorus took the
drowning but not the boy. The town's prayer-stones become the game's
save-obelisks; the first threshold beat fires on his first step out (*"THE FOLD
BEHIND YOU / The chorus begins."*). **No fight happens here** — the sanctuary is
inviolate, and combat is what begins the moment mercy ends.

### 6.2 The Kelp Shelf — bargaining

The drowned climb: ship skeletons standing like a dead forest, mast after mast,
every wreck that ever sank pointing the same way — *up*. This is the region of
bargains with weight and gravity: *"rope enough to reach the light — if you don't
weigh anything anymore."* Its people are the miners who broke into a gallery that
sang back and stayed to listen; listening too long is what turning to salt *is*,
down here. The Shelf teaches Mir the ascent is real and possible, and that
everything here would climb if it could only let go of enough — which is the
lesson he will spend the rest of the game refusing.

### 6.3 The Breach — the wound

The waterline. The one seam in the world where the drowned dark meets the air,
thin as a footstep — thin enough that a small thing can slip through it. Staged
as a carnival that kept performing after the water came, the Breach is where the
sky *"remembers you have a face,"* and does not approve. **This is where Nari is
taken.** On Mir's first step onto the surface the loss beat fires once: the boy
is simply gone, the caption in the echo voice, the sprite fading, the fact
persisted forever on the save (*"NARI? / He was right behind you."*). The
footprint trail that has toddled behind Mir this whole way ends here, at a
scuffle mark in the ground, and does not pick up as two feet again. Everything
after the Breach is *after* the wound.

### 6.4 The Scar — despair *(the long middle: 11 nodes)*

The hostile surface, and the heart of the game — a huge, ruined, monster-strewn
waste of claw gouges and scorch, where *"the surface does not want you; it has
left its instructions, in gouges."* The Scar is where the world got 10× bigger,
and its length is the *point*: despair is long. The search here runs as a real
progression, told in the ground (the v12.2 tracking mystery) — Nari's signature
gait is learnable (paired stride, a heel dot, a faint right-foot drag), and the
Scar is full of prints that are *not* his:

- **The Fresh Trail** *(early Scar).* The prints are still clear and Mir is close
  behind. Hope with teeth. The surface's first wraiths.
- **The False Trails** *(mid Scar).* Pilgrims' paired adult strides pace the
  margins; three-toed den-things cross the waste; single-file decoy prints wander
  and double back. Mir loses the trail and finds it, loses it and finds it —
  *"they walk IN. Toward the den. Why would he walk toward it?"* The dread that he
  has been following the wrong feet for miles.
- **The Den's Mouth** *(late Scar).* The tracks all lead one way now, and none
  lead back — *"that is the only welcome it offers."* A collector's shelf: little
  things arranged by size, a shoe, a comb, a tooth too small to be yours.
  Whatever keeps this place does not *eat* what it takes. It **collects**. This is
  the first Mir understands that his son was not killed on the surface. He was
  *kept* — and the keeper is climbing toward the same Stage he is.

*(Design note: the 11 Scar nodes currently draw one biome's fight pool. These
three sub-stretches are the intended progression for giving that long climb
distinct beats — see §10.)*

### 6.5 The Conductor's Stage — acceptance

A drowned concert plaza and the hall beyond it: toppled statues of an audience,
programs floating, the orchestra that drowned rehearsing the ending and never
stopped. Here is the cage, the Conductor at his stand, and behind the baton,
Lunal, watching. The Stage poses the game's real question as a fight: the world
offers Mir exactly what Lunal chose — *keep* what you love, held forever, unsung
and unchanging, safe in the one place the song can't reach. To win is to refuse
it: to break the Conductor's hold and let the last chord land, knowing that
landing it ends everything, including the stillness that keeps the boy exactly as
he is. Mir chooses rest over keeping. That choice is the ending.

## 7. The untold stories (canonical arena staging)

Each movement is fought inside a specific place staged to imply a past the game
never narrates (PRD §11.1.1). This is the canon behind each staging — **never
surfaced as text in-game**; it exists so the art stays coherent.

- **The Fold (the drowned village).** The village drowned during its harvest
  festival — the ring of boats was a blessing rite for the fleet. The rising
  water came *with* the first verse of the chorus, gently, and most didn't run.
  One boat still strains at its mooring above the green: the baker's daughter cut
  everyone else's boats loose first, and hers ran out of rope. Now the survivors
  pray to the obelisk they woke to, and nobody ever leaves. Mir is the first who
  does.
- **The Kelp Shelf (the salt mine).** The miners broke into a gallery that sang
  back. They gathered at the tunnel mouth to listen — pillar of salt is what
  listening too long *is*, down here (*"when you're turning to salt, it's when
  you're staring ahead"*). The one statue facing away is the foreman, who covered
  his ears and ran, and calcified anyway, mid-stride, three steps from the lift.
- **The Breach (the carnival).** The carnival kept performing after the water
  came — *"cannibals so eager we'll eat each other on dates."* The champion's
  final match was against something that came up through the ring floor; the
  ropes snapped outward when it left with him. This is the waterline, the seam
  thin enough that a small thing slips through. The tipped chairs face away
  because in the end no one could watch.
- **The Scar (the collector's den).** The tracks all lead one way — *in*, toward
  the den — and none lead back. Whatever keeps this place does not eat what it
  takes; it collects, arranged by size. This is where Nari's trail ends at a
  scuffle and where the colder truth waits: something has been walking a small
  pair of prints *toward* the den, and it is not the boy's own choice. (An older
  tenant is staged here too: a room boarded from the inside, where someone locked
  themselves in to transcribe the sound in the walls before it finished
  transcribing them.)
- **The Conductor's Stage (the concert hall).** The orchestra drowned rehearsing
  the ending. The Conductor kept conducting; the stands' pages are blank because
  he erases every attempt except the last row's — the only players who ever got
  it right, and whom he therefore could never let leave. The clocks each stopped
  at the moment a player gave up; they melt because he keeps re-conducting those
  moments. The music falters every time the small one in the cage cries, then
  starts again, angrier. Behind the baton, Lunal watches, and keeps.

## 8. The echoes — the explorable world (PRD §8.8)

The walkable world is five joined regions, one continuous ascent, each dressed in
its arena's visual language so approaching a place foreshadows it. Strewn off the
critical path are **~40 echoes** (8 per region) — hand-placed lore fragments,
each surfacing exactly one line when found (PRD §8.8.2). No echo text is ever
shown automatically; the player must walk to it and interact. Re-voiced to the
ascent (v12.0), they carry four braided threads: **the drowned place's own
grief; the cult's fear of the surface; the cosmology of the chorus; and — from
the Breach up — the trail of Nari and the truth of the one who keeps him.**

- **The Fold** grieves and warns: *"We didn't raise the obelisk. We woke on the
  floor and it was already listening."* — *"Nobody leaves the Fold. It isn't a
  rule. It's just that nobody ever has."* — *"She untied every line but her own."*
- **The Kelp Shelf** is the climb: *"Every ship that ever sank points the same
  way. Up."* — *"Rope enough to reach the light — if you don't weigh anything
  anymore."* — *"The foreman covered his ears and ran. He calcified mid-stride,
  facing away."*
- **The Breach** is the crossing, and the first note of the loss: *"The line
  between worlds is thinner than a footstep. His fit inside mine."* — *"The first
  breath burns. The second one is his name."*
- **The Scar** is the search turning to dread: *"They walk IN. Toward the den.
  Why would he walk toward it?"* — *"It doesn't eat what it takes. It collects."*
  — *"Learn his gait or lose him: paired stride, a heel dot, the faint right-foot
  drag."*
- **The Stage** is the truth of who holds him: *"The music stops every time the
  small one cries. Then it starts again, angrier."* — *"She doesn't hunt to kill.
  She hunts to keep."* — *"The cage is exactly the size of a boy who stopped
  growing when the water came."*

**Secrets (PRD §8.8.3).** Each region hides at least one non-obvious traversal
element — a rock gap that only reads as passable up close, a path behind a wreck
or waterfall, a route visible only from one vantage — gating a relic or an echo,
never critical-path content. Hand-placed, not procedural: a discoverable secret
has to be a decision someone made.

## 9. The ending

Beat the Conductor and the baton comes free of Lunal's hand. Her hold breaks; the
mask clatters where she stood — grief's keeper unmade, revealed as the same
drowned wound wearing a face that could pretend it had chosen this. And with no
one left re-conducting the moment of loss, the chorus does the only thing it has
never been allowed to do: it **lands the last chord.** The unresolved note
resolves. Which means the composition ends — and the world with it — not
destroyed, but at last permitted to rest. Time moves. The water quiets.

The three lines the game actually says:

> *The hall falls silent. A mask clatters where the huntress stood.*
> *Then — not silence. Rain, far above. And under it, small: a laugh.*
> *Nari. Found. The chorus rests.*

The resolution is personal and cosmic in the same chord: the note landing *is*
Nari freed from the cage. Rain far above and a small laugh — the surface, air, a
child. **Found** does not mean un-drowned; it means the two of them at rest,
together, in a world that finally stopped holding everyone open. The game leaves
this exactly as ambiguous as it should be. The world stays open afterward — the
echoes remain findable — so the ending returns Mir to the drowned world rather
than hard-stopping: rest, not erasure.

## 10. Open story threads (design)

- **The Scar's 20-node arc.** After the 10× expansion the Scar carries 11 of the
  20 fight-nodes but still draws a single fight pool, so the long middle plays as
  one enemy repeated. §6.4 defines the intended three sub-stretches (Fresh Trail
  → False Trails → Den's Mouth); wiring distinct foes/echoes/beats to them is the
  next content pass.
- **The Lunal fight (v2, §18).** She is revealed only by staging at the finale.
  A post-Conductor Lunal confrontation — grief-as-keeping made a boss — is the
  strongest v2 story beat.

## 11. Tone & naming

**Tone.** Beautiful-grim. Surreal, poetic, melancholy dread — Blasphemous /
Hollow-Knight register, not splatter. The horror is in the *images* (drowned
clocks, a maw too wide, a cage the size of a child, a trail of small prints
walking the wrong way) — rendered as gorgeous moody painterly 2D. Minimal text;
the world speaks through place, sprite, and song.

**Naming.**

- **Title:** *The Drowned Chorus*
- **Tagline:** *a rhythm of rust and tide*
- **Soundtrack:** six real recorded tracks by the band **Inhalants** (§11.2 of
  the PRD) — *Sunshine Sally*, *Deereater*, *Glassriff*, *John's Anus*, *Truckers
  for Christ*, *Quotience*. (These song titles were briefly used as hero names in
  a retired draft; they are songs, not characters.)
- Prior codename "Project Meterfall" is retired (kept only in git history and
  older PRD revision entries).
